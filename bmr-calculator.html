What is BMR?

Your Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR) is the number of calories your body needs to maintain basic functions — breathing, circulation, cell production — while completely at rest. It accounts for roughly 60–75% of total daily calorie burn for most people.

BMR is not the same as TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure). Your TDEE includes the calories burned through daily movement and exercise on top of your BMR. Eating at your TDEE maintains your current weight; eating below it creates a deficit for weight loss.

This calculator uses the Mifflin–St Jeor equation, which is considered the most accurate formula for estimating BMR in most adults.

Activity multipliers

  • Sedentary (×1.2) — desk job, no exercise
  • Lightly active (×1.375) — walking, light gym 1–3×/week
  • Moderately active (×1.55) — gym 3–5×/week
  • Very active (×1.725) — hard training 6–7×/week
  • Super active (×1.9) — athlete or physical labour

BMR vs TDEE

BMR is your resting calorie floor — the minimum your body needs to survive. TDEE adds everything on top: walking, exercise, even digesting food (thermic effect).

What is BMR and why does it matter?

Your Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR) is the number of calories your body burns each day to maintain its most basic physiological functions while you are completely at rest — breathing, circulating blood, regulating body temperature, growing and repairing cells, and keeping organs functioning. It represents the minimum energy requirement to sustain life and accounts for approximately 60–75% of total daily calorie expenditure for most people.

Understanding your BMR is important because it forms the foundation of any calorie planning strategy. Whether your goal is weight loss, weight gain, or maintenance, you need to know how many calories your body burns at baseline before you can make informed decisions about how much to eat. Eating significantly below your BMR for extended periods is not recommended, as it can lead to muscle loss, nutrient deficiencies, hormonal disruption, and metabolic adaptation.

BMR is influenced by several factors that cannot be changed — age, sex, height, and genetics — as well as factors that can be modified, most notably body composition. Because muscle tissue is metabolically more active than fat tissue, people with higher muscle mass have higher BMRs. This is one of the key reasons why resistance training is beneficial not just for strength and aesthetics but also for long-term weight management.

The Mifflin–St Jeor equation explained

This calculator uses the Mifflin–St Jeor equation, published in 1990 by researchers M.D. Mifflin and S.T. St Jeor. It is widely considered the most accurate formula for estimating BMR in the general population and is recommended by the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics as the preferred equation for clinical and practical use. Multiple studies comparing BMR prediction equations have found that Mifflin–St Jeor most closely matches measured resting metabolic rates across a broad range of ages and body sizes.

The formula uses four variables: weight (in kilograms), height (in centimetres), age (in years), and sex. For men, the equation is: BMR = (10 × weight) + (6.25 × height) − (5 × age) + 5. For women: BMR = (10 × weight) + (6.25 × height) − (5 × age) − 161. The constant at the end (positive for men, negative for women) reflects the average difference in resting metabolic rate between sexes due to differences in body composition.

Even the most accurate formula has a margin of error of approximately 10% compared to laboratory measurements. Individual metabolisms vary due to genetics, thyroid function, medication use, and other factors. Use your BMR estimate as a starting point and adjust your calorie intake based on real-world results over two to four weeks of consistent tracking.

From BMR to TDEE: accounting for activity

Your BMR represents only what you burn at rest. To find your Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE) — the actual number of calories you burn in a typical day — your BMR is multiplied by an activity factor that accounts for how much you move. This calculator uses the Harris-Benedict activity multipliers, which range from 1.2 for sedentary individuals to 1.9 for those with extremely active lifestyles or physically demanding jobs.

One of the most common mistakes in calorie planning is overestimating activity level. Many people who exercise three or four times per week consider themselves "very active," but if they have a desk job and are largely sedentary outside of their workouts, "moderately active" or even "lightly active" is a more accurate description. Overestimating your activity level leads to a higher calculated TDEE and therefore a smaller actual deficit than intended, which can cause frustration when results don't match expectations.

As a practical guideline: if you are not sure which activity level to choose, select one level lower than your instinct suggests. It is easier to adjust upward if you find you are losing weight faster than expected than to discover weeks later that your calorie target was too high. How to increase your BMR over time

While you cannot change your age, height, or genetics, you can meaningfully increase your BMR by building lean muscle mass. Muscle tissue burns approximately 6 calories per pound per day at rest, compared to roughly 2 calories per pound for fat tissue. This difference may seem small, but over the course of a year, even a modest increase in muscle mass can translate into hundreds of additional calories burned per week without any change in activity level.

Progressive resistance training — consistently challenging your muscles with increasing loads over time — is the most effective way to build muscle. Compound exercises such as squats, deadlifts, bench press, and rows recruit large amounts of muscle tissue and produce the greatest metabolic benefit. Training two to four times per week with adequate protein intake (0.7–1g per pound of bodyweight) provides the stimulus and building blocks needed for muscle growth.

Avoiding extreme calorie restriction is also important for preserving your existing metabolic rate. When you eat too little for too long, your body responds by reducing energy expenditure — a process called metabolic adaptation. Maintaining a moderate deficit, taking occasional diet breaks, and prioritising sufficient protein all help minimise metabolic slowdown during weight loss.
